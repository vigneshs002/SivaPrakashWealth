Top 5 Mistakes to Avoid When Choosing Insurance

In 28 years of insurance consulting in Chennai, I have seen the same five errors repeated by well-meaning families again and again. Each one is avoidable. This article walks through them plainly so you can protect yourself before you sign anything.

Mistake 1: Under-insuring the primary earner

The most common — and most damaging — mistake is buying a life insurance sum assured that is far too small. Many people choose ₹5–10 lakh of cover simply because the premium is low. But if the earning member of a family passes away, ₹10 lakh disappears in months when you account for loans, school fees, and daily living costs.

A useful starting rule: life cover should be at least 10–15 times your annual income. A 35-year-old earning ₹6 lakh per year should ideally carry ₹60–90 lakh in term cover — not ₹10 lakh. The premium difference between these amounts is smaller than most people expect, especially if you buy early.

Mistake 2: Ignoring health insurance waiting periods

Health policies always have waiting periods — typically 30 days for general illnesses, 2–4 years for pre-existing conditions, and specific periods for maternity and certain surgeries. Most buyers discover these clauses only at claim time, not when they buy.

Before finalising any health plan, ask specifically: What are the waiting periods? What are the exclusions in the first policy year? If you have a known condition such as diabetes or hypertension, look for insurers who offer shorter waiting periods after underwriting — or accept the loading and disclose fully, so the claim cannot be disputed later.

Mistake 3: Mixing investment with protection without a clear plan

Endowment and money-back plans serve a purpose — but many people buy them as their only life cover, because an agent presents the maturity payout as a benefit. The result is a policy that provides inadequate protection at a high premium, while the returns are modest.

A cleaner approach for most families: buy a term plan for pure protection (low premium, high cover), and if savings or pension accumulation is a goal, assess LIC plans specifically designed for that purpose — Jeevan Umang, Jeevan Labh, or pension products — alongside the right base cover. The two purposes should be separated in your thinking, even if you choose products that combine them.

Mistake 4: Not updating nominees and policy details

I regularly meet clients whose nominee on a 15-year-old LIC policy is still their father — who has since passed away — or a sibling they no longer have contact with. When the policyholder dies or the policy matures, the claim process becomes needlessly complicated for the family.

Review your nominee and contact details every three to five years, or after any major life event: marriage, birth of a child, death in the family, change of address or phone number. This is a five-minute update with your insurer or advisor and can save months of paperwork for your family.

Mistake 5: Buying purely for the 80C tax deduction

Every year before March, many people rush to buy an insurance policy to exhaust their Section 80C limit. This creates two problems: they buy whatever is available quickly rather than what fits their needs, and they stop paying premiums if their tax situation changes — triggering a lapse or surrender at a loss.

Insurance should be bought because you need protection or a structured savings goal, not because of a deadline on a tax return. If 80C is a factor, fine — but let it be a bonus, not the reason. The right plan bought for the right reason will still give you the 80C benefit, without the regret.

What to do instead

  • Start with a needs analysis: How much do your dependents need if your income stops? When do you need the money — next year, in 15 years, at retirement?
  • Separate your protection need (term cover) from your savings or pension need (endowment, annuity, ULIP).
  • Read the policy document — especially the waiting period, exclusion, and claim procedure clauses — before you pay the first premium.
  • Review your portfolio every two to three years with an advisor who will point out what is working and what needs adjustment — not just sell you the next product.
